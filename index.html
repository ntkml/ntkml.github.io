Who I Am

Nthabiseng, but you can call me Nthabi...

The Basics: Nthabiseng Kamala. 22. Aquarius.

The name "Nthabiseng" originates from the South African language Sotho meaning happiness or joy — an energy I try my best to continuously emulate. You can just call me Nthabi, though. I'm proud to be the daughter of two African immigrants and feel empowered carrying my culture through my name.

Moving Mountains Through Stories...

How do you deconstruct 17 years of self hatred?

Being a black woman in America has its fair share of challenges. Growing up as a black woman, the daughter of immigrants, living in conservative, rural Ohio comes with even more layers. Where does one find identity in a community where they don’t even see themselves?

It took me until high school to begin deconstructing the Eurocentric ideals I had been so desperately subscribing to for over a decade. Ideals forced upon me not only by the community I was in but by the media all around me.

For the underrepresented, seeing a person on television who looks like them can have a limiting effect if that person is restricted to behaving only in certain ways, which don’t reflect the breadth of their life’s experience.


Media has an incredible, instantaneous reach with a platform powerful enough to have versatile narratives seen by potentially millions. We choose the stories that are shared — and I want to create content that inspires and excites audiences with representation from all walks of life.
Media has the power to move mountains, spark movements, and elicit change. With 30 seconds, a photo, a design, a slogan — lives are impacted.

And I want to be at the front end of that.

My first job at Boston College was working in the Department of Earth and Environmental Sciences managing the front desk. There are different mediums to perform activism. Through Black Experience in America Through Song (B.E.A.T.S.) I constantly feel empowered by singing black music, with black people, for black people. As president, I structure rehearsals, books rooms and event spaces, and delegate roles to the rest of our executive board.

FACES is an anti-racist organization committed to educating the Boston College community on issues of race, identity and systems of power and privilege as they exist at BC and beyond  . Through my work in this organization I've learned how to facilitate discussions on race in academic settings, organize protests, and to go about my activism in a productive way.
